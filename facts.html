Facts

Seals have highly sensitive whiskers that enable them to hunt effectively even in poor visual conditions. Their specially shaped whiskers can detect the disturbances left by other creatures moving through the water, even while they are moving themselves.
Unlike humans, harbor seals breathe out before diving. They use oxygen already in their blood and muscles while under water, and their heartbeat slows from about 100 beats per minute to 10.
The Hawaiian monk seal is unique in that they live in a tropical climate. Most seals prefer frigid water.
Ribbon seals are the only seals with an internal air sack, which is on the right side of their body over their ribs. It is believed to be used for producing underwater vocalizations.

There are 33 species of pinnipeds today, most of whom are seals. Pinnipeds are categorized into three groups; walruses, "eared seals" or sea lions, and earless seals, known as true seals. Earless seals do however have ears, they're just hidden beneath their skin. Seals come in all sorts of shapes and sizes and are found on every continent on Earth. They range from the giant southern elephant seal, which can weigh more than a pick up truck, to the slender, 100 pound Baikal seal. However, all seals have something in common. Thick layers of fat, known as blubber, keeps them warm, in addition to dense fur. As species also have feet shaped like fins. In fact, the work pinniped means "fin-footed in Latin." These fin-shaped feet make them supreme swimmers and all pinnipeds are considered semi-aquatic marine mammals. This means they spend some part of their lives on land or sea ice, usually during pupping or mating season.

Fossil records indicate that the ancestors of modern seals first entered the ocean on the west coast, about 28–30 million years ago.Seals evolved from carnivorous ancestors that walked on land with sturdy legs; only later did these evolve into the flippers that the family is known for. Seals also have a long history with humans. Thirty-two species of sea lion and seals have been held in captivity with the first captive seals dating back to the Roman Empire. They were displayed around the Empire in travelling circuses or zoological gardens, where they were trained for public entertainment. The display of seals in travelling circuses around Europe continued through to the 1800s with handbills advertising them as “talking fish,” “merman,” or “sea monsters.” In the United States, seals were stars at the country’s first aquarium—the Boston Aquarial Gardens. Ned and Fanny were likely harbor seals and, acquired in 1856, were soon trained to perform complicated tricks like playing a hand organ. In New York, the endangered Caribbean monk seal became the most popular attraction at the New York Aquarium, which housed a number of seals from this species throughout the early 1900s. These seals, Nellie and Tom, were celebrities in the City, with their deaths even reported in the New York newspapers and their remains donated to the collection of the American Museum of Natural History. Today, harbor seals and California sea lions are the most common pinnipeds in zoos and aquariums.

The most famous pinniped in New England was a seal named Andre. In 1961, Harry Goodridge, the harbormaster of Rockport Harbor, Maine, adopted an abandoned harbor seal found in the Penobscot Bay, Maine. Named Andre, the seal chose to stay with Goodridge and for the rest of his life he spent the warm seasons in Penobscot Bay. As a pup, Andre lived in the Goodridge house and would even watch television with the family. Later, he and Goodridge would perform daily shows on the docks for tourists, a tradition that lasted until Andre’s death at 25 years old in 1986. Today, a statue of Andre sits on the shore of Rockport Harbor.

Harp seal pups are born with long white fur that helps them absorb sunlight and stay warm while they’re still developing blubber. Pups shed their white fur after about three to four weeks old.
Seals are trained and utilized by the US Navy and Russian Navy. They are used for underwater object recovery, detecting torpedoes, and carrying tools for divers.
True seals usually swim at about 6 mph and can reach speeds as fast as 18-20 mph.
Seals have large eyes to allow them better vision underwater, particularly at darker depths. On land their vision is greatly reduced.
